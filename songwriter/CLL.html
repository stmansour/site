Some people have expressed concern that Cute Little Lesbians is an offensive song. Without expressing my own opinion or Matt's, I sent out the lyrics to the song to 50 women and asked them if they found the song lyrics to be offensive. The age of the women ranged from 21 to 72. I realize that asking them whether or not the lyrics were offensive will cause them to approach the lyrics with a suspicious eye. But I wanted to know what they thought. After hearing back from all of them I summarized the results in the message below and sent it out to all the participants.


Hi All,

 

Thanks to everyone who responded to my request for comments, thoughts, criticisms, etc. about “Cute Little Lesbians”. This song was written by Matt Kramer and me back in 2008 / 2009. We appreciate the time you took to provide feedback. I wanted to send this summary to let you know that I actually read and thought about **all** of your responses and to explain what we intended to communicate in the song lyrics.

For those who just want the quick summary...

 

Cute Little Lesbians is a love song about a young man who is fascinated by lesbians and who is in love with one lesbian in particular. He fantasizes and daydreams about her. The lyrics reveal that he would never ask her to change, he will always love her, he wants to protect her, and he fantasizes about her accepting him into her life. And in the tradition of rock and pop genres, the song is filled with sexual innuendos.


Cute Little Lesbians is musical art, not a political statement. But in these times when sensitivity to LGBTQ issues is so high, people are very sensitive when they see certain words. Based on many of the comments you provided and the follow-up discussions, it seems as though the use of the term “lesbian” sometimes made people cautious or uneasy. But for the young man described in the lyrics the term “lesbian” is very normal, and in this case, endearing.

The Title: Cute Little Lesbians

The title is the place to start. Some people said that the title is offensive, though it was difficult for anyone to explain why. Here is what I heard, along with some comments.


A couple of people suggested that the word “cute” made it offensive. In the case of the title, “Cute” is used here to describe a girl or woman with a pleasant or appealing face. Perhaps you have shown someone a picture and they said “oh, she’s cute.” That is how we’re using the term in the title.


Several people suggested that the word “little” made it offensive. In this case, “little” is used as a term of endearment. There are many song titles and lyrics that use “little” in this way: “Sweet Little 16”, “ My Little Darling”, “Go Away Little Girl”, “ Little St. Nick”, “Run Little Linda” … and hundreds more.  Think about lyrics where “little” has been used in this way.  From Stevie Ray Vaughn “... she’s my sweet little baby, I’m her little lover boy”; from the Ramones “Hey, little girl I want to be your boyfriend”; from the Everly Brothers “wake up, little Susie, wake up”... the list goes on and on. In all of those examples, “little” is used as a term of endearment. That’s how we’re using the term as well.


It was interesting that no one suggested that the term “lesbian” made the title offensive... at least initially. But in follow-up discussions with some of you, the word “lesbian” clearly made you uncomfortable. A couple of you actually came right out and said so. Since the entire song is about a lesbian it seems reasonable for the term to be in the title. Furthermore, “lesbian” is the term used by this group to identify themselves. It is not one of the derogatory slang terms used to poke fun or to be offensive. The fact that everyone avoided calling out the word “Lesbian”, even though it was the word that caused their uneasiness, suggests just how uncomfortable people are even talking about lesbians.


Several people suggested that the title fetishizes lesbians. I followed up with those folks. If we replace the word “lesbian” in the title with other words, such as “car”, “dog”, “house”, etc., no one thought that it fetishizes.


As an aside, no one said why fetishizing something was offensive. In fact, a couple of feminists pointed out that fetishes themselves are harmless so long as there is no associated oppression, subjugation, and dehumanization. Clearly, in the lyrics of this song, there is no oppression, subjugation, or dehumanization.


Within this sample group, the term “lesbian” made people cautious and uncomfortable. Unfortunately, the only time a lot of people ever hear the term “lesbian” is when it is associated with some sort of human rights violation or prejudice.  So, even when using the term in an innocent or light-hearted way, its association with human rights violations and prejudice issues seems to override everything else. In some of our follow-up conversations, several people admitted that it was simply the word “lesbian” that caused them to interpret the title and lyrics in a negative way.

Specifics In The Lyrics

Most of the comments I received described a more general feeling that the lyrics were offensive or that they might offend others. This makes sense in light of the negative connotations that people have in their minds when they hear the term "lesbian".  In fact, only two of the responses made specific references to lyrics that they thought others might find offensive.

 

The first comment is listed below. It is representative of what many of you said, but it was notable in that it contained specific references:

Yes I feel [it is] offensive. I feel unappreciated [sic] and [that it is] disrespectful toward lesbians. For example wagging her pelvis, just like a guy, most girls don't have the femme... This whole paragraph, etc. The attitude of the song is the same: from a straight man[‘s] angle, to see [a] lesbian in an odd way. The real lesbian will be angry [Grin emoji] but the melody of the song is great!

Let's parse this out:

  • “The attitude of the song is the same: from a straight man angle, to see lesbian in an odd way”. If you mean that it’s odd for a straight man to fall in love with a lesbian, yes that is the whole point of the song! It is an unusual topic, that’s what makes it interesting. There is no reason to view this as offensive. He cannot help what he feels. The song explores his musings about her.
  • “wagging her pelvis, just like a guy”  This tells us about something he finds interesting about this lesbian. Her behavior around cute girls is just like a guy’s. It also suggests that she has a lewd trait, just like some guys have. The fact that he’s thinking about this trait tells us that he likes it, or at least he is curious about it.
  • “The real lesbian will be angry”. We’ve played this song to two lesbians to get their reaction. They were not offended. That said, these were people we knew. The reaction might have been different if we didn't know them.

    But just for a moment, set aside everything else and just consider the arc of the story in these lyrics: A young man falls in love with a lesbian... nothing offensive here. He is not in love with her in order to offend lesbians, he simply can't help what he feels. The young man has sexual musings about someone he’s fallen in love with, that’s what we as human beings do... again, no reason to take offense here.

The only other specific reference to the lyrics was related to the second verse:


Most girls just don’t have the stuff
to play the part of the femme fe tough
She wonders sometimes, what it is like
to have what I have, and have it all night
To flash it, and wield it, admire it and feel it
and plunge it like a knife


Lot’s of sexual innuendo here (welcome to the world of pop-music lyrics). Here is the specific comment: "the part where she says she wants what I have and she wants it all night is problematic. It makes people think that lesbians are lacking." Think about all other things this young man has revealed in the lyrics. He said he will never ask her to change, he’ll always love her, he’ll protect her, he’ll be there for her when everyone else runs away. These are not the thoughts of a young man trying to find fault with a lesbian. He loves her. He is simply a curious young man, and he wonders what she thinks about sexually.
